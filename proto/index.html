Cartesian Plane

In my maths class, I wanted to visualise the linear functions that we were studying without using a tedious pen and paper, so I created a <canvas> program to plot them for me! This started off as a small test app, but it's recently become my most worked-on project.

Using the mouse, you can scroll in, out and around the plane to explore the functions. The plus and minus buttons on the side scroll without a mouse wheel, while the button in the middle centres the pan. To view more detailed functions, turn off the "Fast Rendering" option at the bottom of the plane area.

Click on the functions and they hide themselves - click again and they re-appear. Scroll down to the bottom and you will find a text box and some options. The text box can be used to input new equations and the options can be used to change the behaviour when you click on the functions. Enter pi for the ratio between circumference and diameter, x^y for exponents and sin, cos and tan are available as trigonometric functions. For polar equations, enter a in place of x. For regular equations, the f(x) = is not required, but polar equations require f(a) = to be recognised properly.

BUGS/ISSUES:

  • Some particular zoom and pan configurations cause the scale algorithm to fail and loop forever. This has been caught with an error handler, but will need a better solution in production.

    Complex Plane

    After watching a few introductory videos on complex numbers and how they could generate fractals, I decided to implement a few complex number functions in JavaScript. This is the result.

    To generate a fractal (in this case, the Mandelbrot set), simply click on the blue "Generate" button at the bottom of the area. If you want to see more of the fractal, simply bump up the "Maximum Iterations" value. After a point (specifically >600) it starts to become ludicrously slow to generate, so I've stopped it there.

    Speaking of speed, you can decrease the quality by clicking on one of the "Quality" buttons. This will help with speed problems once you start exploring the fractal further.

    You can center the view anywhere on the screen. Simply click on your new center in the fractal area. To zoom in, increase the "Zoom Factor" by around 1. You can also view the path that the complex numbers take by toggling the "Line tracking" feature.

    "Switch Mode" allows you to toggle between Mandelbrot and Julia sets. With Julia sets, you can adjust the real and imaginary components to get different fractals. "Reset Settings" does what you think it does.

    Don't forget, to see your changes in action, click the "Generate" button. However, clicking on the fractal to set the pan values is immediate, so you might have to wait a bit for the fractal to generate.

    tXtEng

    Version 0.2.1

    After seeing the joy of command line interface games and controls, I decided to create a platform that would make it easier to develop these kind of programs for the Web. At the moment, it is in early alpha, so it's featureless. At the moment (v0.2.1), I have implemented:

    • A suitable command line interface
    • Message Of The Day system
    • Command based control protocol
    • The ability to print to the console (print command)
    • Framework for programs (run command) and a test program (flip)
    • A few other utility commands (cl, help)

    NOTES:

    • Unlikely to ever be worked upon again, because it's kinda hard to make a fully functioning terminal emulator in JavaScript.
